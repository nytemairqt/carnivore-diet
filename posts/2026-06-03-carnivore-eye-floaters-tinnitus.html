Can the Carnivore Diet Affect Eye Floaters and Tinnitus? What the Community Says

A Reddit user new to the diet asked about eye floaters, tinnitus, glycine, omega-3s, and dairy all in one post. Kind of a lot for one thread. Here's what people actually report.

June 3, 2026

Eye floaters and tinnitus aren't the first things people think about when they start the carnivore diet. But a Reddti user whos new to the diet asked about exactly these things alongside questions about glycine, omega-3s, dairy, mental health, and constipation. Seven questions in one post. That's a lot for someone whos just starting out.

But they're good questions. The kind that come up more often than you'd think once you've been on carnivore for a while. The the kind nobody puts in the beginner guides because they're too specific. So let's tackle them.

Eye Floaters: Do They Go Away?

The short answer is: some people report their eye floaters disappearing or becoming significantly less noticeable after a few months on carnivore. It's not universal, but it comes up often enough in the community that it's worth paying attention to.

The theory is inflammation related. Eye floaters are caused by tiny clumps of collagen fibers in the vitreous humor of your eye. They cast shadows on your retina, which you perceive as little specks or cobwebs drifting across your vision. Inflammation can make them worse by causing changes in the vitreous or by making your brain more sensitive to them. Reduce systemic inflammation and the floaters may become less noticeable over time.

Carnivores gut heals, their immune system calms down, and the inflammatory load drops. Some people say their floaters faded around the 4-6 week mark. Others say it took a few months. A few people see no change at all. Worth trying if floaters are bothering you and you've already ruled out retinal issues with an eye doctor.

One caveat: if you suddenly see a shower of new floaters, especially with flashes of light, see an ophthalmologist immediately. That could be a retinal tear or detachment and it's a medical emergency. Carnivore doesn't fix detached retinas.

Tinnitus: Does the Ringing Stop?

Tinnitus is another one where people report mixed results, but the success stories are compelling enough to mention. Some carnivore eaters say their tinnitus went away completely within a month. Others say it got quieter. Some see no improvement.

The connection here is probably inflammation and food sensitivities. Tinnitus is often linked to inflammation in the auditory pathway or the eustachian tubes. Some people have food sensitivities that cause fluid buildup or inflammation in the ears without them realizing it. Dairy, gluten, soy, and grains are common culprits. Remove all of them with carnivore and you might find your ears quiet down.

There's also a blood sugar angle. Low blood sugar and blood sugar swings can trigger tinnitus in some people. Carnivore stabilizes blood sugar completely, which might explain why some people's tinnitus fades after a few weeks of adaptation.

Works great for some people. Not everyone. But the pattern is consistent enough that it's worth a 30-day trial if tinnitus is affecting your quality of life.

Glycine and Omega-3s: Are You Getting Enough?

The Reddit poster also asked about glycine and omega-3 intake. Smart question. Most people on carnivore eat mostly muscle meat, which is high in methionine but low in glycine. That's a problem if you're not eating connective tissue, skin, or bone broth.

Glycine is important for collagen production, detoxification, sleep quality, and balancing out the methionine load from muscle meat. Traditional carnivore cultures ate nose to tail - they got plenty of glycine from tendons, cartilage, skin, and bones. Modern carnivores who only eat ribeye and ground beef might not get enough.

The fix is simple: eat more connective tissue. Chicken skin, pork skin, beef tendons, bone broth, or a collagen supplement. A scoop of collagen powder in your coffee or bone broth covers the gap easily. Some people make their own gelatin from scratch. However you do it, getting enough glycine makes a noticeable difference in how you feel. Better sleep, less joint pain, and smoother skin are the most common reports.

Omega-3s are a different story. Ruminant meat, especially grass-finished beef, has a decent omega-3 content but not as much as fatty fish. If you're eating grain-finished beef, the omega-3 content is lower and the omega-6 content is higher. The ratio matters more than the absolute amount.

Most carnivore eaters don't worry about omega-3s unless they have a specific reason to. But if you're concerned about your fatty acid profile, adding some fatty fish like salmon or mackerel a few times a week is an easy way to balance it out. Some people also take cod liver oil or eat fish eggs (roe/caviar) as a whole-food source.

Dairy: Yes or No?

Dairy is a personal choice on carnivore. Some people include it liberally. Others avoid it completely. Both are valid.

The people who include dairy usually stick to butter, ghee, hard cheeses, and heavy cream. These are low in lactose and casein, the two components that cause problems for most people. Butter is almost pure fat with trace protein. Hard cheeses like cheddar or parmesan have most of the lactose fermented out. Heavy cream is mostly fat with minimal protein.

The people who avoid dairy do so because it causes inflammation, digestive issues, mucus production, or skin problems. Some people who thrive on meat and eggs alone find that dairy stalls their progress. If you're doing carnivore as an elimination diet for autoimmune issues, most protocols recommend cutting dairy for at least 30 days before testing reintroduction.

The bottom line: dairy is optional. Try it. See how you feel. Drop it if you notice issues. You don't have to decide on day one.

Mental Health and Constipation: The Short Versions

The poster asked about mental health improvements and constipation too. Both of these have been covered in more detail elsewhere, but here's the quick version.

Mental health: many people report significant improvement in anxiety, depression, and brain fog after 3-6 weeks on carnivore. Stable blood sugar, lower inflammation, and ketones as a clean brain fuel are the main drivers. The first week can be rough mentally as your brain adapts to running on fat instead of glucose. Push through it. The carnivore and mental health post has the full breakdown.

Constipation: some people get constipated at first because they're not eating fiber and their gut microbiome is shifting. This usually resolves on its own within a week or two. Eating enough fat helps keep things moving. So does staying hydrated. If you're still backed up after two weeks, try adding more fat or drinking warm water with salt in the morning. Most people find their bowel movements become less frequent but more comfortable once they're adapted - once a day or even every other day with minimal straining is normal on carnivore.

The Takeaway

All seven questions this Reddit user asked have one thing in common: they're about the body adjusting to a radically different way of eating. Eye floaters, tinnitus, glycine balance, omega-3 ratios, dairy decisions, mental health, and digestion are all signals from a body that's going through adaptation. Some things get better. Some things take time. Some things require you to adjust how you're eating within the carnivore framework.

That's the thing about this diet that nobody tells you upfront. It's not just "eat meat and everything fixes itself." There's a learning curve. But the people who stick with it and dial in their approach tend to find answers to questions they didn't even know they had.
