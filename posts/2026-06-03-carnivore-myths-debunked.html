Common Carnivore Diet Myths Debunked: What the Evidence Actually Shows

Every diet has its myths, and the carnivore diet might hold the record. Let's look at the most common ones and see what holds up.

June 3, 2026

If you've told anyone you're trying the carnivore diet, you've probably heard the same objections on loop. "You'll get scurvy." "Where's the fiber?" "It causes colon cancer." "Isn't it super expensive?" "Doesn't it get boring?"

These objections come up so often they've basically become the standard script for why carnivore can't work. And if you're new to this way of eating, they can be genuinely unsettling. They sound reasonable. They sound like things a doctor or a nutritionist might say.

The problem is most of them are based on assumptions that don't hold up when you actually look at how the human body works. Let me walk through each one.

The Scurvy Myth: "You'll Get Vitamin C Deficiency"

This is the most common one and it sounds terrifying. No fruit, no vegetables equals scurvy, right? Except that's not how vitamin C works in the absence of carbs.

The real story is that vitamin C and glucose compete for the same transport pathways in your body. When you're eating a high-carb diet, your cells have to compete with glucose to absorb vitamin C. That means you need more dietary vitamin C to maintain adequate levels. On a zero-carb diet, your utilization of vitamin C becomes much more efficient because there's no glucose competing for those transporters.

Fresh meat contains enough vitamin C to prevent deficiency - about 1-2 mg per 100g in muscle meat and significantly more in organ meats. You don't need much to avoid scurvy. The minimum daily requirement to prevent deficiency is around 10 mg and you can hit that with 500g of fresh meat. Liver and kidney are even higher. A single serving of beef liver has more vitamin C than an apple.

Historical populations like the Plains Indians, the Inuit, and various European pastoral groups lived for generations on meat-heavy or all-meat diets without scurvy. The idea that humans need constant plant foods for vitamin C is a modern myth that came from the assumption that everyone eats carbs.

It also helps that carnivore diets are naturally high in organ meats. If you're eating muscle meat only, you're probably fine. If you add liver even once a week, you're getting more than enough.

The Colon Cancer Myth: "Red Meat Causes Cancer"

This one has the most cultural weight behind it because the WHO classified processed meat as a Group 1 carcinogen back in 2015. But the details of that classification matter a lot more than the headline suggests.

Group 1 in the WHO classification system doesn't mean "definitely causes cancer at normal intakes." It means "there's enough evidence to say it's carcinogenic under some conditions." For comparison, alcohol is Group 1. So is sunlight. So is working as a painter. The classification tells you about the quality of the evidence, not the size of the risk.

The actual risk increase from red meat consumption in the epidemiological data is small - in the range of 17-18% for processed meat and even smaller for unprocessed red meat. And those studies are almost all observational, meaning they can show correlation but not causation. People who eat a lot of processed meat also tend to smoke more, drink more, exercise less, and eat fewer vegetables overall. The studies try to control for these factors but they can never fully untangle them.

More importantly, the mechanism proposed for colon cancer involves heme iron and N-nitroso compounds, both of which are influenced by the presence of other foods in the gut. When you eat meat with vegetables that contain nitrates and fiber, the chemical reactions are different than when you eat meat alone. The carnivore diet removes those variables entirely. And the anecdotal evidence from people who've had colonoscopies after years on carnivore shows remarkably clean colons with no polyps.

Is this definitive? No. But the claim that red meat definitively causes colon cancer on a carnivore diet is a lot weaker than people assume.

The Fiber Myth: "You Need Fiber for Regularity"

This might be the most interesting one because the evidence for fiber being essential is actually pretty thin when you look past the headlines.

Fiber does help with regularity on a standard diet. It adds bulk to stool and helps things move through faster. But the assumption that everyone needs fiber for healthy digestion ignores the fact that fiber is plant matter - and plant matter irritates some people's guts pretty badly.

On a carnivore diet, your digestion changes completely. You're eating almost nothing that needs to be "bulked up" and pushed through. Meat is almost completely digestible - your body absorbs around 95% of what you eat. There's very little waste left over. People on carnivore often go from daily bowel movements to once every 2-3 days and that's normal. It doesn't mean they're constipated. There's simply less to eliminate.

The Inuit and other traditional meat-based cultures had the same pattern. Infrequent bowel movements that were small and didn't require straining. That's not constipation - that's efficiency.

If you're used to the standard diet pattern of three daily fiber-fueled movements, the carnivore pattern can feel wrong. But your body adapts. And a lot of people with IBS, Crohn's, and ulcerative colitis find that removing fiber actually fixes their digestive problems rather than making them worse.

The Expensive Myth: "You Can't Afford This Diet"

This one depends entirely on how you shop. If you're buying ribeye steaks from Whole Foods for every meal, yes, it's expensive. But nobody who's been doing this for a while eats like that.

The carnivore diet can be very affordable if you know what to buy. Ground beef is the workhorse - it's cheap, versatile, and nutrient-dense. Chuck roast, brisket, pork shoulder, whole chickens, eggs, and canned fish all cost far less per pound than the premium cuts. A pound of 80/20 ground beef costs around $4-5 and provides about 1,200 calories. That's competitive with any other food source on a per-calorie basis.

Buying in bulk from a local butcher, getting a quarter cow or half pig from a farm, and shopping sales at grocery stores all make a big difference. The carnivore on a budget guide covers the full strategy with specific cuts, prices, and freezer management tips.

It's also worth considering what you're not buying anymore. No vegetables, no fruit, no grains, no legumes, no cooking oils, no snacks, no alcohol, no coffee creamer, no bread, no pasta, no rice. When you add up the grocery bill for a standard household and subtract everything that's not animal-based, the cost difference is a lot smaller than you'd think. Some people actually spend less.

The Boring Myth: "You'll Get Tired of Eating the Same Thing"

This one is partly true. You will eat fewer different ingredients on carnivore. The variety in a standard diet comes from plants and processed foods - different grains, different vegetables, different sauces, different spices. On carnivore, your protein sources are meat, fish, eggs, and dairy. That's a shorter list.

But the range of what you can do with those ingredients is wider than most people realize. Different cooking methods produce completely different meals. A slow-cooked brisket tastes nothing like a seared ribeye. Pan-seared salmon is different from oven-baked salmon. Scrambled eggs are different from hard-boiled eggs. Raw dairy has its own flavor profile that changes how you use it.

Seasoning plays a role too. Most carnivore purists use salt only, but plenty of people use herbs, spices, and zero-carb condiments. If variety matters to you, you can have it within the constraints of the diet. You just won't have the infinite variety of a standard grocery store.

And honestly? A lot of people find they don't miss the variety. Once your food stops being entertainment and becomes fuel, you stop caring about novelty. You find a few meals that work and you rotate through them. It's not boring - it's freeing. You spend less time thinking about food, less time cooking, less time cleaning. That trade-off is worth it for most people.

The Sustainability Myth: "You Can't Do This Long Term"

This is the objection that comes up when someone has run out of specific arguments. It usually means "I can't imagine doing this, so nobody can." But the evidence from the people who've been doing it for years says otherwise.

The carnivore community has people who've been eating this way for 5, 10, even 20+ years. Dr. Shawn Baker has been doing it since 2016 and is in his late 50s with better blood work than most people in their 30s. Mikhaila Peterson has been on carnivore for almost a decade and reversed severe autoimmune issues. These aren't outliers - they're the visible examples of a much larger group.

The question of long-term sustainability comes down to two things: nutrient adequacy and quality of life. On nutrient adequacy, the carnivore diet provides all essential amino acids, all essential fatty acids, all fat-soluble vitamins, most B vitamins, zinc, selenium, iron, and enough vitamin C in fresh meat. The only potential gap is vitamin C if you're eating only dried or heavily processed meat, but that's easy to avoid.

On quality of life, the people who stick with it long-term are the ones who feel dramatically better on the diet. They're not white-knuckling it through cravings for bread. They genuinely don't want to go back. When eating plants makes you feel worse - joint pain, brain fog, bloating, skin issues - the motivation to stay carnivore is built in.

The carnivore and mental health guide covers how the diet affects brain function and mood stability, which is a big part of why people stick with it long-term.

The Heart Disease Myth: "Saturated Fat Clogs Arteries"

This is the biggest one and it deserves its own deep dive, which the cholesterol and heart health post covers in detail. The short version is that the saturated fat-heart disease hypothesis has been crumbling for years.

The original research from Ancel Keys in the 1950s was flawed by design. Subsequent systematic reviews and meta-analyses have found no significant association between saturated fat intake and cardiovascular disease. The diet-heart hypothesis was never proven - it was promoted so aggressively that it became conventional wisdom despite weak evidence.

On the carnivore diet, most people see their triglycerides drop to very low levels, their HDL go up, and their LDL go up or stay the same depending on genetics. The LDL increase concerns doctors who aren't familiar with the context, but the overall lipid profile - especially the triglyceride-to-HDL ratio - usually improves significantly. And it's worth noting that LDL particle size tends to shift toward the larger, more buoyant pattern on low-carb diets, which is associated with lower cardiovascular risk than small, dense LDL.

What All This Adds Up To

The myths around the carnivore diet share a common thread: they all assume that what works on a standard diet applies the same way on a zero-plant diet. But human metabolism doesn't work like that. When you remove carbs, the way your body handles vitamin C, fiber, fat, and even cancer risk factors changes in ways that make most of these arguments irrelevant.

That doesn't mean the carnivore diet is right for everyone. It's a restrictive elimination diet and some people won't enjoy it or won't tolerate it. But the specific objections people throw at it - scurvy, cancer, no fiber, too expensive, too boring, not sustainable - don't stand up to scrutiny. They're based on assumptions that don't apply in the context of a zero-plant diet.

If you're considering carnivore, the real questions to ask are simpler. Do you have a health condition that might improve with dietary intervention? Are you willing to give it a solid 30-day trial before judging it? Can you access affordable animal foods where you live? Those are the things that actually matter. The rest is just noise.
