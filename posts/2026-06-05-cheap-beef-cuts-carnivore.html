How to Choose the Best Cheap Cuts of Beef for Carnivore

Not all cheap beef is created equal. Here's what to buy, what to avoid, and how to cook the budget cuts worth eating.

June 5, 2026

You're on a budget. You head to the store, grab a chuck roast or a shoulder roast because it's half the price of ribeye, and you get home excited for a good meal. Then you cut into it and find rubbery, floppy bits that look like blood vessel roots running through the meat. They slap you in the face when you bite in. Suddenly your cheap dinner isn't appetizing anymore.

This happened to a Reddit user on r/carnivore recently and the frustration was real. They bought a batch of shoulder roasts and every single one was packed with those unpleasant textures. It's a legit problem when you're trying to eat carnivore on a budget.

So what are those bits, which cuts have fewer of them, and how do you cook the cheap ones that are actually worth your time?

What Those Rubber Bits Actually Are

First, they're not really blood vessels. What you're seeing and feeling is a mix of lymph nodes, veins, connective tissue, and the tough sheaths that surround muscle groups. In shoulder cuts especially, the cow uses those muscles a lot, so there's more connective tissue holding everything together. That's also why shoulder roasts have that rich beef flavor when cooked right - all that collagen breaks down into gelatin.

The problem is, most people don't slow-cook shoulder roasts long enough. If you cook a chuck roast the same way you cook a ribeye, you're going to have a bad time. Those rubbery bits stay rubbery. You need to give them time to break down.

But honestly, some cuts are just worse than others. And if the texture ruins your appetite, no amount of cooking time is going to fix the psychological damage of biting into something that feels like a rubber band.

Which Cheap Cuts to Buy

Not all budget cuts are created equal. Here are the ones worth your money and the ones to think twice about.

Best: Chuck Roast

Chuck comes from the shoulder area, so it has plenty of connective tissue. But it also has great marbling and flavor. The trick is cooking it low and slow. A chuck roast cooked in a slow cooker or Dutch oven at 275F for 3-4 hours will be fork-tender with the collagen fully rendered into rich, silky gelatin. The rubbery bits go away. What you get is basically poor man's brisket. It's one of the best value cuts on the entire animal.

Best: Sirloin Tip and Bottom Round

These are leaner cuts from the hind leg. They have fewer of those unpleasant textures because the muscle groups are larger and more uniform. The tradeoff is less fat, so you'll need to add fat when cooking - butter, tallow, or a pat of fat on top. They're great for roasting or slicing thin for stir-fry. Not as flavorful as chuck, but the texture is far more consistent.

Good: Brisket

Brisket is cheap per pound but requires serious cooking time. We're talking 8-12 hours low and slow. The payoff is incredible but it's a weekend project, not a weeknight dinner. Worth learning how to do right. A properly cooked brisket has zero rubbery bits - just tender, fatty meat that tastes like beef squared.

Hit or Miss: Shoulder Roast

This is the cut that caused the Reddit user's frustration. Shoulder roasts can be great, but they're the most variable. Some are beautifully marbled with minimal connective tissue. Others look like a roadmap of veins. If you can see thick white or dark stringy bits running through the meat at the store, put it back. Look for roasts with even color and minimal visible connective tissue lines.

Skip: Stew Meat (pre-cut)

Those little cubes they sell as "stew meat" are usually trimmings from all over the animal - the parts the butcher couldn't sell any other way. You get the worst of every texture in one bag. Buy a whole chuck roast and cut it yourself. Better meat, better price per pound, and you control what goes in the pot. Kind of a no-brainer once you do the math.

How to Cook Tough Cuts So They're Actually Good

The number one mistake people make with cheap cuts is cooking them like steak. High heat, quick sear, medium-rare. That works for ribeye and strip steak. It does not work for chuck, brisket, or round. Those cuts need time.

Here's the simple formula:

Low temperature + long cooking + moisture = tender meat.

You have three good options:

Slow cooker. Set it and forget it. Chuck roast on low for 8 hours with some salt and a splash of water or bone broth. Comes out shreddable. The fat renders into the liquid and you get a rich, beefy result that's nothing like the rubbery mess you were afraid of. Works great.

Pressure cooker. Instant Pot style. A chuck roast that would take 4 hours in the oven is done in 60-90 minutes. The high pressure forces collagen to break down fast. This is the best option for weeknight cooking when you forgot to plan ahead.

Dutch oven. Sear the roast on all sides in tallow or butter, add a little liquid, cover, and put it in a 275F oven for 3-4 hours. Check at 3 hours. The meat should pull apart with a fork. If it doesn't, give it another 30 minutes.

Salt generously before cooking. Like, more than you think. Cheap cuts can handle it and the salt helps break down proteins. Some people salt a chuck roast and let it sit in the fridge overnight before cooking. If you have the foresight, it makes a difference.

Also important: slice against the grain. This matters more for round cuts than chuck, but it always helps. Slicing against the grain shortens the muscle fibers so they're easier to chew. Slice with the grain and even the most tender roast will feel tough.

What About Ground Beef?

If you're really on a tight budget, ground beef is the answer. No connective tissue surprises, no rubbery bits, no guesswork. 73/27 or 80/20 ground beef is the most consistent, cheapest, and easiest protein on the carnivore diet. You can cook it in 10 minutes, you know exactly what you're getting, and it tastes great with just salt.

The Reddit user who posted about the shoulder roasts might have been better off just buying ground chuck. You miss out on the variety, sure, but you never have to worry about biting into something that ruins your appetite.

The Bottom Line

Cheap cuts of beef can be fantastic on the carnivore diet. You just need to know which ones to buy and how to cook them. Chuck roast in a slow cooker is the single best value meal in all of carnivore. Sirloin tip and bottom round work well for roasting. Brisket is the weekend project that pays off.

And if all else fails, ground beef never lets you down.
