Regular Ground Beef vs Wagyu: When to Splurge and When to Save on Carnivore

A Reddit user made burger patties from wagyu ground beef and regular 73/27 side by side. Added seasoning and cheese. Both were delicious. They decided to stick with the cheap stuff and save wagyu for steaks. Were they right?

June 13, 2026

A post on r/carnivore caught my eye that was refreshingly honest. A user named IDoNotHide made burger patties from both wagyu ground beef and regular 73/27 ground chuck. Seasoned them the same, added cheese, ate them side by side, Verdict: both were delicious. They said they'll stick to the cheap stuff and save wagyu for steaks.

This is the kind of real-world testing that actually helps people. Not a lab study, not a influencer pitch, just someone cooking dinner and paying attention. And it raises a good question: is expensive ground meat ever worth it on carnivore? Or is ground beef one of those things where the cheap option is just as good?

Let's break it down.

Why People Buy Wagyu Ground Beef

First off, wagyu ground beef isn't the same thing as a wagyu steak. When you buy wagyu ground beef, you're getting the trimmings and offcuts from wagyu carcasses that don't make it into steak form. It's not like someone took a perfect A5 ribeye and ran it through a grinder. So there's a quality range even within "wagyu ground beef" that depends on what grade of wagyu the trimmings came from and what ratio of fat to meat was used.

People buy it for two reasons. One, they think it'll taste noticeably better. Two, they think the fat profile is superior - higher monounsaturated fats compared to standard grain-fed beef, which is the whole thing wagyu is famous for. And technically, both of these are true to some degree. Wagyu does have a different fat composition. And well-sourced wagyu trimmings can produce ground beef that tastes richer than standard chuck.

The question is whether the differene is big enough to justify paying 2-3x the price.

The 73/27 Argument

Regular 73/27 ground beef is the workhorse of the carnivore diet for a reason. It's cheap, it's fatty, and it tastes like beef. The 27% fat content is actually ideal for carnivore - you get a solid fat-to-protein ratio that keeps you full and provides enough energy without needing to add extra tallow or butter to every meal.

When you form that into patties, season with salt, and cook them in their own rendered fat, you get a burger that satisfies. Add cheese and it's hard to tell the difference from something that cost three times as much. That's basically the OP's experience in a nutshell.

There's also something to be said for consistency. 73/27 from a decent grocery store is predictable. You know what you're getting. Wagyu ground beef can vary wildly depending on the source. Some of it is amazing. Some of it is just regular ground beef with a premium label and a higher price tag.

When Wagyu Ground Beef Makes Sense

I'm not going to say wagyu ground beef is never worth it. There are situations where it makes sense.

You're not adding cheese or strong seasoning. The OP added seasoning and cheese. That masks a lot of subtle flavor differences. If you eat your burgers plain - just meat and salt - the higher quality of wagyu trimmings becomes more noticeable. The beef flavor is cleaner, the fat renders more smoothly, and the texture is slightly different.

You can afford it and it fits your budget. If money isn't tight and you want the best possible burger, go for it. Nobody's stopping you. The carnivore diet is expensive enough without feeling guilty about treating yourself. But be honest about whether you can actually taste the difference in a blind test.

You care about fat composition. Wagyu fat has a higher proportion of monounsaturated fats compared to standard corn-fed beef. Some people feel better on higher monounsaturated fat profiles. If you've noticed you feel different eating wagyu vs commodity beef, that might be why. It's not just marketing.

For variety. Eating the same 73/27 day after day gets boring. Splurging on a pack of wagyu ground beef once in a while breaks the monotony. That has value even if the nutritional difference is small. Sustainability on carnivore is about more than just macros.

When to Stick With Regular Ground Beef

Most of the time, the cheap stuff is fine. Here's when to save your money.

You're cooking burgers with toppings. Cheese, eggs, maybe some bacon on top - all of these overpower whatever subtle differences the wagyu brings. You're paying extra for a nuance that gets buried under everything else.

You're meal prepping in bulk. If you're making a big batch of ground beef for the week, don't buy wagyu. The flavor difference diminishes when you reheat it, and you're throwing money away. Save the fancy stuff for a special meal.

You eat ground beef every day. For daily staple eating, 73/27 is the right choice. It's affordable, consistent, and nutritionally solid. Wagyu ground beef as a daily driver is a fast track to an empty wallet.

You're on a tight budget. This is the big one. Carnivore is expensive. If you have to choose between wagyu ground beef and being able to afford enough food, the answer is obvious. Ground beef gets the job done. A $5/lb chuck roast stretched across a few meals is way more valuable than a $15/lb wagyu burger that disappears in five minutes.

What the OP Got Right

The OP's conclusion - "I think ill stick to the cheap stuff and save wagyu for steaks" - is probably the right call for most people. Wagyu shines as a steak where you can actually appreciate the marbling, the texture, and the flavor without anything getting in the way. Ground beef is ground beef. The grinding process homogenizes everything and reduces the differences between cuts and grades.

There's a reason even high-end restaurants use regular ground beef for their burgers. They might blend cuts, add fat trimmings, or grind in-house, but they're not using wagyu striploin for burger meat. Because it's a waste. The qualities that make wagyu special are lost when you grind it up and cook it well-done.

Does that mean you should never buy wagyu ground beef? Nah. If you want it and can afford it, go for it. But know what you're paying for. You're paying for a marginal improvement in flavor that most people won't notice once cheese and seasoning are involved. And that's fine if you're okay with that trade.

For daily eating, 73/27 is the move. Save the fancy stuff for when you actually want to taste the meat.
